1. For clinicians — what this does, in plain terms

This page describes the rules a piece of decision-support software uses to decide whether a 30-minute CTG trace deserves your attention right now. You don’t need to read the code; this section is for you.

What the software is trying to do

Every 30 minutes, it looks at the trace and produces two separate things:

  • A guideline category (1 / 2 / 3) — reassuring, indeterminate, or abnormal — exactly the way the guideline you picked (FIGO, NICE, ACOG, or SOGC) would classify the trace.
  • An alert level — none, warning, or critical — answering a different question: “should someone look at this now?”

Why keep the alert separate from the category

The middle tier of every guideline — “indeterminate” or Category II — is enormous; a large fraction of all traces in any labour ward live there. If every Category II rang a bell, the bells would never stop and you’d learn to tune them out. So the software keeps the category honest to the guideline, and only escalates the alert when something more specific is going on: a long deceleration, a persistent flat trace, recurrent late dips, a worsening trend, a sinusoidal pattern, or a stack of clinical risk factors that the trace alone wouldn’t justify.

The short version: category = what the guideline calls it. Alert = whether you should walk over to the bedside.

What raises the alert

Roughly in order of how strongly each one pushes towards critical:

  • A single severe acute event. A prolonged deceleration ≥ 5 min, or a suspected sinusoidal pattern, will reach critical on its own.
  • An abnormal guideline category (Category 3 / pathological / abnormal) on its own already meets the critical threshold.
  • Recurrent worrying patterns — recurrent late decelerations, complicated variable decelerations.
  • Baseline abnormalities — tachycardia > 160 bpm (more weight if > 180), bradycardia < 110 bpm (more weight if < 100), or a baseline that is steadily rising over the epoch.
  • Reduced variability that has lasted a while — < 5 bpm for 30 min or more, with more weight at ≥ 50 min.
  • Tachysystole (> 5 contractions per 10 min) — but only if the toco channel is good enough to trust the count.
  • Deterioration compared to the previous epoch — a Category 1 → 2 or 2 → 3 transition adds weight; an improving trace gets a small reduction.
  • Clinical risk factors you supply — oxytocin, meconium, fever, sepsis, prolonged ROM, pre-eclampsia, diabetes, growth restriction, prematurity, previous caesarean, slow progress. None of these alert on their own, but they lower the bar so an otherwise “borderline” trace tips into warning earlier in a higher-risk context.
  • Degraded (but still usable) signal quality — a small nudge upward, never a reason to reassure.

The safety guarantees you can rely on

  • A bad signal never produces an “all clear.” If the FHR trace is too poor to classify, the software refuses to assign a category and the alert is forced to warning with a “review the trace” concern. It will never silently reassure you on unreadable data.
  • Category 3 is never silent. If the trace is graded abnormal, the alert will be at least warning even if the rest of the scoring would have suggested otherwise.
  • A long deceleration is caught by duration alone. If toco is missing or degraded so we can’t cleanly call a dip “late” or “variable,” a sustained drop (≥ 2 min) is still flagged on duration. No silent misses because of toco quality.
  • Unreliable toco never drives escalation. If toco quality is poor, tachysystole is still reported as “possible, low-confidence” for your review, but it is not allowed to push the alert level up.
  • Every concern carries its evidence. Each finding lists what it is, when in the epoch it started, how long it lasted, and the numbers behind it — so you can independently confirm the basis of any alert.

When to trust the alert less

  • Low confidence. The result lists a confidence field of high, medium, or low. A low-confidence result means the signal was usable but degraded; treat the alert as a prompt to look, not as a verdict.
  • Possible sinusoidal pattern. True sinusoidal patterns are rare and false positives exist. The software flags them aggressively (critical) precisely because you need to be the one who confirms or rules them out.
  • Tachysystole “low-confidence (toco).” The count is suspect; treat as a hint to check toco placement, not as a count you can act on numerically.
  • Anything you would not have called yourself. The software is decision support. It does not diagnose, treat, or replace clinical judgement; your read of the trace and the clinical picture overrides it.
Regulatory note. ctgkit is not a cleared medical device. It is built to make a regulated path defensible (auditable outputs, explainable findings, human-in-the-loop intent), but the rules described here have not been validated against annotated clinical data and must not be used as the sole basis for a clinical decision.

2. The mental model: category vs. alert

ctgkit produces two independent verdicts on every epoch:

OutputQuestion it answersWho decides
category (1 / 2 / 3) What does the chosen guideline call this trace? (reassuring / indeterminate / abnormal) The guideline pack you picked (FIGO, NICE, ACOG, SOGC).
alert (none / warning / critical) Is escalation justified right now? A separate, guideline-agnostic scorer that weighs persistence, acute events, trend, signal quality, and clinical risk factors.

The split is deliberate, and it’s the single most important design idea to understand. The middle tier of every guideline (especially ACOG “Category II”) is enormous — a huge fraction of all traces land there. If every Category 2 fired a page, clinicians would be buzzed constantly and start ignoring the system. The category stays faithful to the guideline; the alert only escalates when escalation is actually justified.

Rule of thumb. The guideline pack changes the category far more than it changes the alert. The alert scorer is the same regardless of pack.

3. End-to-end scoring flow

One call to score_and_alert(category, features, quality, meta, prev) does this:

  1. Short-circuit on bad signal. If FHR quality was too poor to assign a category, return warning immediately with a single concern. We never silently “reassure” on unreadable data.
  2. Add a base score from the guideline category.
  3. Add per-finding contributions for acute events, recurrent patterns, baseline, variability, contractions.
  4. Compare to the previous epoch and adjust for trend (worsening / improving / stable).
  5. Stack clinical risk factors from the supplied metadata dict.
  6. Bump for degraded (but still usable) signal quality.
  7. Floor the score at 0, compare to thresholds, choose the alert level.
  8. Apply the safety floor: Category 3 (abnormal) can never produce none.
  9. Sort concerns by severity (high → moderate → low → info) so the most important one renders first.

5. Thresholds & safety floors

4.1 The two thresholds alerts.py:17–18, 222–228

Score rangeAlert level
score < 40none
40 ≤ score < 100warning
score ≥ 100critical

The score is floored at zero before comparison, so an improving trend on an otherwise clean trace can’t produce a negative score.

4.2 Safety floors

  • Bad signal never reassures. No category ⇒ forced warning. alerts.py:47–56
  • Category 3 never silences. If the score somehow lands below 40 on an ABNORMAL category, the alert is bumped up to warning. alerts.py:231–232

4.3 Worked example

An indeterminate trace, recurrent late decels, oxytocin running, signal a bit degraded:

category = INDETERMINATE         +30
n_recurrent_late > 0            +35
oxytocin in metadata             +12
quality.low_confidence           +10
                                ----
                          score = 87  →  WARNING  (40 ≤ 87 < 100)

Add a prolonged ≥ 2 min decel on the next epoch (+40) and the trace tips to critical at 127.

6. How to modify the alert strategy (technical)

The scorer is intentionally one file, with named constants and a single function. Tuning is local.

5.2 Common tuning recipes

Make the system less chatty — raise the warning cutoff:

# ctgkit/alerts.py
WARNING_THRESHOLD = 50      # was 40
CRITICAL_THRESHOLD = 110    # was 100

Give a site more weight on oxytocin (e.g. for induction wards):

_RISK_WEIGHTS = {
    ...
    "oxytocin": 20,         # was 12
    ...
}

Add a new clinical risk factor — one dict entry; the metadata dict will be inspected automatically:

_RISK_WEIGHTS = {
    ...
    "twins": 10,            # new
}

# Caller can now do:
ctgkit.analyze(sig, metadata={"twins": True})

Add a brand-new rule — one branch inside score_and_alert():

if f.some_new_feature:
    score += 15
    concerns.append(Concern(
        "my_new_rule", "Human-readable title",
        Severity.MODERATE, "one-line explanation",
        supporting_channels=["fhr"],
        evidence={"value": f.some_new_feature},
    ))

If the rule needs data that isn’t in Features yet, also add it to extract_features() in ctgkit/features.py.

5.3 Things to be careful with

  • Don’t remove the safety floors. The “no category ⇒ warning” short-circuit and the “ABNORMAL never silent” bump are load-bearing. Multiple tests pin them in place — see tests/test_behaviour.py.
  • Keep the acute-event branch as if/elif/elif. They’re mutually exclusive on purpose so a single decel doesn’t double- or triple-count.
  • Tachysystole on low-confidence toco must not contribute to the score. Removing the tachysystole_low_confidence branch would let unreliable contraction counts drive escalation.
  • Re-run the test suite after any tuning: 
    python -m pytest -q
    Behaviour tests assert the exact level (none / warning / critical) on canonical synthetic traces; if you’ve genuinely changed the policy, update those tests in the same commit so the new policy is the asserted contract.

5.4 Versioning a policy change

The EpochResult records library_version and pack_version on every output for audit. When you change a weight or threshold, bump ctgkit/version.py (and pyproject.toml) so downstream consumers can tell that a result produced today wasn’t generated under the same policy as one produced last month.
